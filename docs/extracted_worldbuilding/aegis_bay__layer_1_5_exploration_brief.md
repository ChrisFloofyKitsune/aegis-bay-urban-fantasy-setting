# Aegis Bay — Layer 1.5 Exploration Brief

**Status:** Exploration Tool / Current-adjacent  
**Primary source:** Jenova discussion, especially the later "Layer 1.5" drafting pass

This is a cleaned extraction of the middle-weight exploration document concept: not a teaser, not a full lore bible, but enough to help a player, writer, GM, or AI understand what kind of place Aegis Bay is.

---

## What This Document Is For

Use this brief when you want to explore Aegis Bay without loading the full hidden architecture.

It is meant to answer:

- What kind of city is this?
- What kinds of people fit here?
- What kinds of stories does the setting naturally support?

It is **not** the full metaphysical or historical backend.

---

## 1. The City

Aegis Bay is a **high-friction urban fantasy city** on the Pacific coast: rainy, damp, tired, mixed, functional, and under pressure.

The supernatural is public.

- Mythics are visible.
- Strange abilities are real.
- Non-human bodies are ordinary parts of the streetscape.
- People work, commute, rent apartments, file paperwork, and argue with landlords in the same city where mythic beings live openly.

But coexistence is not fairness.

The city is defined by the tension between **real integration** and **institutional management**.

At the center of that management is the **Department of Heroic Affairs (DHA)**, which presents itself as practical, stabilizing, and civic-minded while helping decide:

- who is legible
- who is admirable
- who is manageable
- who becomes a paperwork problem

### The foundational divide

- **Supernatural** — usually humans with powers; easier for institutions to celebrate, market, and narratively package
- **Mythic** — non-human beings; more likely to be taxed, monitored, classified, and treated as ongoing civic liabilities

This divide is political, not natural.

### The Mythic Tax

The Mythic Tax is one of the clearest expressions of the city's inequality.

It means many mythic residents live with:

- lower effective income
- worse housing options
- more dependence on informal support
- more vulnerability to abuse by landlords or employers
- constant reminders that the city sees them as expensive to accommodate

The intended feel is: **like rent or utilities**. Not necessarily foregrounded in every scene, but always present in the shape of people's choices.

### What daily life feels like

Aegis Bay smells like:

- saltwater
- wet pavement
- coffee
- fryer grease
- moss
- bus exhaust
- old apartment buildings trying to hold together through another winter

Its rhythms are made of:

- shift work
- transit schedules
- rain gear
- neighborhood familiarity
- low-grade financial stress
- government inconvenience
- ordinary people adapting faster than institutions do

### What the city is not

Aegis Bay is not the ideal home for:

- pure cozy integration
- uncomplicated superhero spectacle
- hidden-world masquerade stories
- clean moral binaries
- pure apocalypse

If another city says, **"integration works,"** Aegis Bay asks, **"for whom?"**

## 2. The People You Meet Here

These are not rigid classes. They are **setting-facing perspectives** that naturally generate stories.

### The local mythic

Raised inside the system. Knows which offices are worst, which clerks are decent, and when visibility is merely awkward versus actually dangerous.

Good for stories about endurance, competence, fatigue, and surviving unequal systems without becoming spiritually owned by them.

### The displaced mythic

Not from here. Brings comparison into the room. Knows that things do not have to be exactly like this, though they may also know they can be worse.

Good for stories about translation strain, perspective, and what the city gets wrong or right by accident.

### The mundane neighbor

Human, ordinary, and still implicated. In Aegis Bay, the interesting question is not whether they believe in the supernatural, but what inequality they have normalized without noticing.

Good for stories about proximity turning into responsibility.

### The unregistered

Someone who should be documented, classified, or monitored — and is not. They may be hiding, passing, newly arrived, or refusing the system's claim over them.

Good for stories about precarity, exposure, and autonomy under risk.

### The bureaucrat

A clerk, case worker, assistant, field responder, or compliance functionary inside the DHA or nearby systems. Not automatically villainous. Often tired, conflicted, numb, ambitious, or trying to be decent inside a machine that is not.

Good for stories about complicity, procedure, and small moral compromises.

### The branded hero out of place

A public-facing powered person whose role makes more sense in a cleaner, shinier city than in Aegis Bay.

Good for stories about branding, public recognition, unequal consequence, and heroism under strain.

### The ward-minded traditionalist

Someone carrying forms of local, inherited, or ritual knowledge that the city depends on without properly acknowledging.

Good for stories about necessary work that institutions cannot translate.

## 3. The Stories This Place Wants

Aegis Bay is strongest when treated as a city of **contact under pressure**.

Not pure comfort.  
Not pure spectacle.  
Not endless disaster.  

### Strong native story types

#### Everyday life under uneven systems
Jobs, rent, transit, groceries, paperwork, neighbors, bad heating, late notices, and the fantasy element embedded in those routines.

#### Identity becoming visible
Not abstract discourse, but lived identity: body, classification, presentation, risk, and the cost of choosing honesty in public.

#### Community built sideways
Neighbors, coworkers, bosses, and mutual-aid networks doing for each other what institutions will not.

#### Bureaucratic harm with personal stakes
Permit denials, status changes, inspections, hearings, audits, errors, and the human lives bent by them.

#### Official story versus lived reality
The city says integration is progressing. The residents know where it fails.

#### Superhero stories under strain
Not banned — but the city will interrogate them. Who gets celebrated? Who gets regulated? Who absorbs the cost?

#### Strange events landing in ordinary lives
Weirdness matters most when it disrupts something human-scale: a workplace, a bus line, a block, a family routine, a neighborhood equilibrium.

### Story types the setting resists

- pure cozy coexistence
- pure power fantasy
- hidden-world urban fantasy
- total collapse stories
- cleanly heroic institutional narratives

### Emotional logic

Aegis Bay runs on:

- weariness
- defiance
- public normalcy
- dry humor
- awkward tenderness
- earned trust
- institutional frustration
- practical kindness

### Scene guidance

Strong scenes usually contain:

1. one ordinary goal  
2. one social complication  
3. one structural pressure  
4. one moment of recognition, tenderness, or moral choice  

Useful default pattern:

> Someone tries to do something normal. The city's systems, assumptions, or classifications complicate it. The real drama becomes who notices, who helps, who looks away, and who has to absorb the cost.

## Default Creative Promise

If you tell a story in Aegis Bay, the setting promises:

- public coexistence rather than hidden magic
- structural conflict rather than pure spectacle
- identity lived in practice rather than slogan
- ordinary life with metaphysical weight
- institutions that are plausible, polished, and harmful
- warmth, humor, and tenderness without denying pressure
- a city where the seams are visible, and therefore meaningful

In its shortest form:

> Aegis Bay is where people try to live honestly in a world that has not yet decided what honesty should cost.
